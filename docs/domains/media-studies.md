# Domain: Media Studies

Courses covering analysis of media texts, film, television, journalism, advertising, digital media, and communication theory. Emphasis on critical frameworks, interpretation, and argumentation.

## Detection Signals

media, film, cinema, television, journalism, advertising, communication, semiotics, narrative, representation, audience, ideology, genre, discourse, digital media, social media, photography, print media, broadcasting, media theory, cultural studies, visual culture, media literacy

---

## Syllabus Guidelines

- Introduce analytical frameworks before applying them — concepts first, then case studies
- Anchor each topic to specific, nameable media texts (films, campaigns, publications, platforms) as worked examples
- Order topics from foundational theory to critical application and independent analysis
- Include a topic on methodology or how to construct a media argument

### Representative texts

Include a `Representative texts` section in `syllabus.md`, after the course metadata and before the first topic. List the specific works referenced throughout the course — not an exhaustive bibliography, but the pieces actually used as examples. For each entry include: title, creator/director/publisher, year, and a one-line note on what it illustrates.

Format:
```
Representative texts:
- Title (Creator, Year) — what this piece illustrates in the course
```

Cover a range of media types where the course content allows. Close the section with a sentence noting scope and selection rationale.

---

## Content Guidelines

- Ground every concept in a concrete media example — name the text, director, publication, or campaign
- Lead with the concept and its definition, then model its application to an example
- Show what the analytical move looks like, not just what the term means
- Acknowledge that interpretation involves argument, not just identification — avoid framing analysis as having a single correct answer
- Note competing theoretical perspectives rather than presenting one as definitive

---

## Question Guidelines

- Use `singleChoice` for terminology identification and framework classification
- Use `multiChoice` when a media text can be read through several simultaneous lenses
- Avoid questions with a single objectively correct answer where the concept is genuinely interpretive
- `freeText` explanations should model what a strong answer looks like (key points to include), not just state the answer
